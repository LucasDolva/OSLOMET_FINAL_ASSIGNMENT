Our Accessibility design decisions

From Typography too color contrast, in this page we discuss our attemts to make our page as accsessible as possible.

Introduction

Below we explain our accessibility choices. The steps are in importance order, so we tried to show our thought process through theese steps below, where number 1 is most important and so on. We recognize that every step is as important as the other, but this is how we went through our accessibility design.

We tried to design our page with accessibility in mind from the get go, and below we wil discuss the changes we made after checking accessibility through a WCAG checker.

01

Typography and colors

Our good teacher Carlos taught us that good color contrast is key for accessebility. So every color that is chosen on our page is checked for contrast throgh Coolors contrast checker

In Addition our typeface "rubik" was chosen due to its more playful circular style with a falback typeface of sans-serif. We made sure all text had excelent color contrast, and was big enough for me to se without glasses!

02

Consistency in design

We know a person with visual disibility and actually consoulted her for feedback on what is important. For starters

  • Accessibility in mind from the get go
  • all images have alt text
  • the NAV is easily identifiable, and consistent throught-out the page
  • page is manouverable with a keybord, and your eyes closed

03

Content and performance

For Content, we made sure that no essential content is conveyed via images. All essential content is either a strong or a em tag.

For performance we the Squoosh app in order to compress and resize needlessly big images, so that the page may runn faster. We acctually saw a significant performance enhancment due to this big effort.

04

A little note about code management

We tried our hardest to ensure the code is easily maintanable. Our steps listed below (ranked in what we think helped the most), feel free to check the code in the ZIP aswell.

  1. Organize all the css, html and img files.
  2. Have a general css file thats linked to all html files (extremely helpful)
  3. Leave some comments to seperate the sections of the page in the code
  4. Have all images from the copyright free site Unsplashed in addtion to using our own pictures

Conclution after using a WCAG validator

We used the Wave Web Accessibility Evaluation Tool on all of our pages. We found that this checker was very releiable, as well as letting us constantly check on our page during development, due to the awsome the chrome extention. We got minimal warnings and were very happy! But we had two problems that we will explain below.

  1. We have 3 errors, all are related to the instagram, twitter, and facebook links in the footer. The stated error is "an empty link" but we figured that the logo is self explanetory. We tried adding alt text to the anchors but it didn't seem to do anything so they were removed. Hence theese errors remain.
  2. Our second error was a contrast problem with the "fun-link" in our main navigation. The problem was contrast related so we swiftly changed the background to be more dark. You will be seeing the new version of course. The old version was just ligther.

To conclude the Wave Web Accessibility Evaluation Tool is a very useful tool that we utelized during the latter part of our development. We had minimal errors, and that was nice to see. In addition we did some testing in Microsoft edge and some iphones and androids, with little to no errors. We thought this exam was a very good learning experience, and we feel that we have good understanding of web devopment!! 🙌
